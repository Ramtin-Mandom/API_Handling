Certainly! Below is a structured summary and critical analysis of the paper **“Evaluating the Implementation of Energy Adaptive Reuse in Historic Buildings: A Case Study of the Historical Raghadan Hotel in Al Tal”** by Yathreb Sabsaby and Dalia Majzoub.

---

### Introduction

This paper investigates **adaptive reuse (AR)** as a sustainable strategy for rehabilitating historic and heritage buildings, focusing specifically on the abandoned Raghadan Hotel in Tripoli, Lebanon. The research addresses pressing challenges such as poor thermal comfort, inefficient energy use, and inadequate daylighting prevalent in neglected historic structures. The goal is to explore how sensitive architectural interventions can improve environmental performance without compromising heritage integrity. The study contributes a **performance-driven, simulation-based framework** tailored to the Lebanese Mediterranean context, aiming to balance climate resilience, sustainability, and cultural conservation.

---

### Key Concepts

- **Adaptive Reuse (AR):** The process of repurposing obsolete buildings for new functions while preserving historical and architectural authenticity, integral to sustainability and circular economy principles.
- **Heritage Conservation & Sustainability:** Balancing heritage values with contemporary energy standards, in line with international charters like the Venice Charter (1964) and the Historic Urban Landscape (HUL) approach.
- **Energy and Environmental Performance:** Emphasis on energy consumption, thermal comfort, daylighting, and carbon emissions.
- **Passive Interventions:** Non-invasive methods such as skylights, insulation, and stone finishes that enhance building performance.
- **Building Performance Simulation (BPS):** Use of software tools like DesignBuilder (based on EnergyPlus) coupled with **infrared thermography** for diagnostics and predictive assessments.

---

### Methodology

- **Case Study:** The 200 m² abandoned Raghadan Hotel located in Al Tal, a dense urban district in Tripoli characterized by decay and abandonment of historic structures.
- **Simulation Framework:**
  - Baseline ("Before") model created as an unoccupied, uninsulated shell to replicate actual degradation.
  - Simulations accounted for real **local climatic data**, adjacent urban obstacles, and microclimatic effects relevant to Mediterranean conditions.
  - Performance metrics captured energy use, thermal comfort, daylighting, and CO2 emissions.
- **Proposed Interventions:**
  - Central skylight to enhance daylighting.
  - Roof insulation with terracotta tiles to reduce solar heat gain.
  - Stone interior finishes to increase thermal mass.
- **Tools:**
  - Infrared thermography for envelope diagnostics.
  - DesignBuilder for dynamic energy modeling before and after interventions.
- **Data Analysis:** Comparative evaluation of energy demand, thermal stability, daylight levels, and carbon footprint.

**Methodological Evaluation:**

- **Strengths:**
  - High-fidelity, localized simulation incorporating real microclimatic and urban barrier data.
  - Integration of diagnostic infrared thermography with dynamic energy modeling—a robust mixed-method approach.
  - Focus on passive, reversible interventions respects heritage conservation principles.
  - Quantitative performance benchmarks offer replicability.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - Baseline model simplification as a fully abandoned shell may not reflect semi-occupied or intermittently used real-life scenarios.
  - The study focuses only on passive interventions, omitting active renewable technologies or HVAC upgrades.
  - U-values for traditional materials were partly estimated from literature rather than validated by physical testing, potentially limiting accuracy.
  - Single case study limits generalizability beyond Al Tal’s specific urban and climatic context.

---

### Results

- **Energy Efficiency:**
  - Total Source Energy reduced from 16594.26 kWh to 16117.06 kWh.
  - Total Site Energy reduced from 10983.26 kWh to 10506.06 kWh.
  - Energy intensity per building area improved slightly (~3 kWh/m² reduction in conditioned area).
- **Thermal Comfort:**
  - More stable interior temperatures due to enhanced thermal mass and insulation.
  - Decreased peak cooling loads and mitigation of thermal instability.
- **Daylighting:**
  - Central skylight significantly improved natural light penetration, reducing artificial lighting dependency.
- **Carbon Emissions:**
  - CO2 emissions decreased consistently across the year, with a relative peak reduction of 6.22% in winter.
- **Visualizations:**
  - Thermal imaging simulations showed reduction in heat gain on roofs and external walls.
  - Cooling load duration and intensity significantly lowered, particularly in hot months.
- **Qualitative Findings:**
  - Proposed interventions achieved a balance between improved environmental performance and architectural integrity.
  - Enhanced building envelope respected heritage guidelines, such as being passive, reversible, and easily identifiable.

---

### Conclusion

The study demonstrates that **heritage-sensitive passive adaptive reuse interventions can considerably improve energy performance, thermal comfort, and carbon footprint** in historic buildings without compromising architectural preservation. It bridges the gap between sustainability and heritage conservation, providing a replicable, data-driven framework suitable for contexts similar to Tripoli’s historic urban fabric. The results advocate adaptive reuse as not only a conservation strategy but also a means for urban revitalization and climate change mitigation in Mediterranean heritage cities.

---

### Critical Analysis

**Strengths:**

- **Comprehensive Multidisciplinary Approach:** Combines conservation theory, sustainability, and advanced simulation, incorporating local climate and urban context.
- **Robust Simulation and Diagnostic Integration:** The coupling of infrared thermography with energy modeling lends rigor and specificity to performance diagnostics.
- **Practical Passive Designs:** Interventions are realistic, low-cost, and respect historical authenticity.
- **Clear, Quantified Outcomes:** Empirical data demonstrate measurable benefits, improving credibility.
- **Locally Relevant:** Addresses an underexplored region (Lebanon) with unique architectural and climatic challenges.

**Weaknesses:**

- **Limited Scope of Interventions:** Exclusion of active renewable systems or modern HVAC leaves potential gains unexplored.
- **Baseline Simplification:** Modeling an abandoned shell assumes the worst-case scenario, but many historic buildings may have partial occupancy or different usage patterns influencing performance.
- **Material Data Assumptions:** Reliance on literature for thermal properties may reduce result precision; lab testing would enhance model reliability.
- **Single Case Study:** Findings, while thorough, are restricted in generalizability without multiple or comparative case studies.
- **Economic and Social Dimensions Not Explored:** No assessment of cost-effectiveness, occupant behavior, or community impact, limiting holistic sustainability understanding.

**Reliability of Results:**

The results appear **reliable within the study’s simulation context**, given the use of validated software (DesignBuilder/EnergyPlus) and real climatic inputs. Infrared thermography adds diagnostic accuracy for envelope weaknesses. However, assumptions about material properties and baseline condition introduce uncertainty. The passive interventions’ impact is consistent with established building physics knowledge, reinforcing confidence in outcomes. Yet, actual on-site measurements post-retrofit would be essential for full validation and replication.

---

### Overall Assessment

The paper robustly addresses its research question with a well-structured methodology linking heritage conservation to measurable environmental improvements. It contributes meaningfully to the somewhat limited literature on sustainable adaptive reuse in Mediterranean heritage contexts. While some methodological simplifications and scope limitations exist, the study’s **strength lies in its interdisciplinary, simulation-driven approach offering practical, heritage-sensitive retrofit guidelines**.

Future research would benefit from expanding to multiple case studies, integrating active renewable systems, enhancing material property data with empirical testing, and incorporating economic/social impact analyses for comprehensive sustainable adaptive reuse strategies.
